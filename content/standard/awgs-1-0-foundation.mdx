### Version 1.0

### Status
Public Draft Standard

# Executive Summary

The increasing adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) agents introduces a new class of digital actors capable of discovering information, interacting with services, making decisions, and performing actions on behalf of individuals and organizations.

Existing web and interoperability standards primarily focus on human users, search engines, and accessibility technologies. They do not comprehensively address the requirements necessary to support trustworthy interactions between autonomous agents and digital systems.

The Agentic Web Governance Standard (AWGS) establishes a vendor-neutral framework for enabling safe, transparent, interoperable, accountable, and trustworthy interactions between AI agents and digital services.

AWGS provides principles, governance domains, compliance levels, and assessment guidance that organizations may use to prepare systems for the emerging Agentic Web.

---

# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Purpose

The purpose of AWGS is to define a common governance framework for interactions involving autonomous AI agents and digital systems.

AWGS seeks to:

- Promote safe and responsible agent interactions.
- Improve interoperability across agent ecosystems.
- Increase transparency and accountability.
- Support trust and provenance.
- Encourage privacy-preserving practices.
- Establish measurable compliance criteria.

## 1.2 Vision

The vision of AWGS is:

> To establish a trusted, transparent, and governed Agentic Web where autonomous agents and digital systems can interact safely, responsibly, and effectively.

---

# 2. Vendor Neutrality

AWGS is a vendor-neutral standard.

Compliance with AWGS shall not require the use of any specific:

- Vendor
- Platform
- Product
- Framework
- Protocol
- Technology

Organizations may implement AWGS using technologies appropriate to their operational environment.

Examples of supporting technologies may include OpenAPI, OAuth, OIDC, MCP, Agent-to-Agent protocols, or future interoperability standards.

---

# 3. Scope

## 3.1 In Scope

AWGS applies to:

- Websites
- APIs
- SaaS platforms
- Web applications
- Agent platforms
- Agent-accessible services
- Digital services
- Machine-readable services
- Agent-to-agent interaction services

## 3.2 Out of Scope

AWGS 1.0 does not directly address:

- Robotics
- Autonomous vehicles
- Industrial control systems
- Consumer IoT devices
- Embedded systems

Future versions may expand coverage.

# 4. Core Principles

The following principles guide the design and implementation of AWGS.

## P1 - Discoverability

Capabilities should be discoverable and understandable by agents.

## P2 - Interoperability

Digital systems should support interoperable agent interactions.

## P3 - Transparency

Actions, decisions, and outcomes should be explainable.

## P4 - Accountability

Agent actions should be traceable and auditable.

## P5 - Trust

Identity, provenance, and authenticity should be verifiable.

## P6 - Privacy

Personal and sensitive information should be processed responsibly.

## P7 - Safety

Digital systems should implement safeguards against harmful or unintended agent behavior.

## P8 - Human Oversight

Digital systems should provide mechanisms for human review, intervention, escalation, or override when significant consequences may arise from agent actions.

# 5. Governance Domains

AWGS requirements are organized into governance domains.

## D1 Discoverability

Focuses on machine-readable discovery of services and capabilities.

Examples:

- Capability descriptions
- Service catalogs
- Structured metadata
- Machine-readable documentation

## D2 Interoperability

Focuses on communication and integration between agents and systems.

Examples:

- Service interfaces
- Standardized interaction mechanisms
- Capability exchange
- Protocol compatibility

## D3 Identity

Focuses on identifying agents and services.

Examples:

- Agent identity
- Service identity
- Credential management
- Delegated identity

## D4 Authorization

Focuses on permissions and delegated authority.

Examples:

- Access scopes
- Least privilege
- Delegated permissions
- Purpose-based access

## D5 Transparency

Focuses on explainability and visibility of actions.

Examples:

- Decision explanations
- Policy explanations
- Action explanations

## D6 Accountability

Focuses on traceability and auditability.

Examples:

- Audit logs
- Event records
- Provenance tracking
- Accountability records

## D7 Trust

Focuses on establishing confidence in interactions.

Examples:

- Verification mechanisms
- Reputation indicators
- Authenticity validation
- Source trustworthiness

## D8 Privacy

Focuses on responsible handling of information.

Examples:

- Consent
- Data minimization
- Purpose limitation
- Privacy controls

## D9 Safety

Focuses on operational safeguards.

Examples:

- Risk assessment
- Human approval workflows
- Escalation mechanisms
- Rate limiting
- Operational constraints

# 6. Compliance Levels

AWGS defines three levels of compliance.

## Level A - Agent Ready

The system provides sufficient information for agents to discover and understand available capabilities.

**Primary Objective:** Agent Awareness

## Level AA - Agent Interoperable

The system supports secure and governed interaction between agents and digital services.

**Primary Objective:** Agent Interaction

## Level AAA - Agent Governed

The system demonstrates mature governance, trust, transparency, and safety controls.

**Primary Objective:** Agent Trustworthiness

---

# 7. Conformance

Organizations may claim AWGS conformance only when:

- Mandatory requirements are satisfied.
- Required governance controls are operational.
- Required documentation exists.
- Compliance is maintained during the claim period.

Partial implementation shall not be represented as full compliance.

Organizations should clearly state the level of compliance being claimed.

Examples:

- AWGS Level A
- AWGS Level AA
- AWGS Level AAA

# 11. Normative and Informative Content

## Normative Content

Normative content defines requirements necessary for compliance.

Normative statements typically use terms such as:

- SHALL
- SHALL NOT
- MUST
- MUST NOT

## Informative Content

Informative content provides:

- Guidance
- Examples
- Explanations
- Recommendations

Informative content is not mandatory for compliance.

# 12. Glossary

## Agent

A software entity capable of performing actions on behalf of a user, organization, or another system.

## Autonomous Agent

An agent capable of making decisions and performing actions without direct human intervention.

## Capability

A function, action, or service that may be invoked by an agent.

## Delegation

The assignment of authority from one entity to another.

## Digital Service

A system, application, website, API, or platform accessible through digital interfaces.

## Identity

Information used to uniquely identify an agent, service, or actor.

## Provenance

Information describing the origin and history of an action, decision, or piece of information.

## Trust Signal

Evidence that contributes to confidence in the authenticity, reliability, or integrity of an entity.

## Human Oversight

The ability for humans to review, intervene, approve, reject, or override agent actions.

The Agentic Web represents a significant evolution in how digital systems interact.

By adopting AWGS, organizations can prepare for the next generation of AI-enabled interaction while promoting responsible innovation and public trust.
